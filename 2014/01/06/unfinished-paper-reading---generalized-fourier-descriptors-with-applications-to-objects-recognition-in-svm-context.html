We will denote by \(M_{2,n}\) the set of translations and rotations of a discrete angle. Namely, \(M_{2,N}=\mathbb R^{2}\rtimes\breve N\), where \(\breve N = \{ 2\pi k/N\mid k=1,\ldots,N\}\) endowed with the semidirect product \((X,\theta).(Y,\alpha)= (R_{\theta}Y+X,\theta+\alpha)\).

Representations of \(M_{2,N}\) and GFT

The dual space \(\hat M_{2,N}\) is the union of \(\mathbb Z/N\mathbb Z\) (the set of charachters) and \(\mathcal S = \{ \lambda r\mid \lambda>0\) and the angle $ α(r)∈[0,2π/N)\}$. The support of the Plancherel measure is only \(\mathcal S\)

Any representation in \(\mathcal S\) admits a representative in the class of unitary transformation over \(\mathcal l^{2}(\breve N)\). oNamely, given \(\varphi:\breve N\to\mathbb C\), the representation \(T_{r}\) acts by1 \[ [T_{r}(X,\theta).\varphi](u) = e^{i\langle r,R_{u}X \rangle} \varphi(u+\theta). \]

The Fourier transform (GFT) is then an isomorphism between \(L^{2}(M_{2,N})\) w.r.t. its Haar measure \(d\mu\) and \(L^{2}(\hat M^{2,N})\) w.r.t. the Plancherel measure. We can easily find the expression

\begin{multline} \label{eq:fourier} [\hat f(r).\varphi](u) = \int_{M_{2,N}} f(X,\theta) [T_{r}((X,\theta)^{-1}).\varphi](u)\,d\mu \\ = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N} \int_{\mathbb R^{2}} f(X,\theta)\, e^{-i\langle r, R_{u-\theta} X\rangle}\, \varphi(u-\theta)\,dX. \end{multline}

Observe that \(\hat f(r)\) is an Hilbert-Schmidt operator on \(\mathcal l^{2}(\breve N)\).

Our aim is to study the (weak) completeness of the following set of Fourier descriptors2 (that can be defined for any group with the Chu duality property): \[ I_{1}^{r}(f) = \hat f (r)\circ \hat f(r)^{*} \qquad I_{2}^{r_{1},r_{2}} (f) = \hat f(r_{1}\hat\otimes r_{2})\circ\hat f(r_{1})^{*} \hat\otimes \hat f(r_{2}) ^{*}. \] Here, \(r_{1}\hat\otimes r_{2}\) denotes the equivalence class of (Kronecker) Hilbert thensor products of the representations \(r_{1}\) and \(r_{2}\), and \(\hat f(r_{1})\hat\otimes\hat f(r_{2})\) is the Hilbert tensor product of the Hilbert-Schmidt operators \(\hat f(r_{1})\) and \(\hat f(r_{2})\).

Lifts

We are interested in studying images, which will be represented by compactly supported square integrable functions \(f:\mathbb R^{2}\to[0,1]\). Thus, to compute the Fourier descriptors it is first necessary to lift them to functions in \(M_{2,N}\). We can do this in various ways

Trivial lift

The simplest thing to do is just to define \(f_{t}(X,\theta)=f(X)\) for any \(\theta\in \breve N\). Unfortunately this does not work. Indeed, if we compute the Fourier transform through \eqref{eq:fourier}, we get

\begin{multline} [\hat f_{t}(r).\varphi](u) = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N}\int_{\mathbb R^{2}} f(X) e^{-i\langle r,R_{u-\theta}X\rangle} \,dX\,\varphi(u-\theta) \\ = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N} \tilde f(R_{\theta-u} r)\,\varphi(u-\theta) = (\tilde f(R_{\cdot }r),\bar \varphi)_{\mathcal l^2}. \end{multline}

Here we denote by \(\tilde f\) the Fourier transform over \(\mathbb R^2\) of \(f\).

The previous formula proves that, in this case, the Fourier transform is indeed a projection and as such has rank \(1\). Hence, the crucial point in the proof of the weak completeness, namely that almost all the \(\hat f(r)\) are invertible, cannot be satisfied.

Cyclic lift

Let3 for \(f:\mathbb R^{2}\to \mathbb R\), \[ av(f) = \int_{K} f(X)\,dX, \qquad centr(f) = \int_{K} X f(X)\,dX, \] be the average and the center of \(f\), respectively.

The cyclic lift of a compactly supported function \(f\in L^2(\mathbb R^2)\) is defined as \[ f_c(X,\theta) = f\left(R_{\theta}X+\frac{centr(f)}{av(f)}\right). \]

  • Observe that \(centr(f)/av(f)\) is the "baricenter" of the image, and thus \(f_c(\cdot,0)\) is the centered image.
  • The cyclic lift can be defined only for non-zero average functions (if we restrict to images, i.e., functions with values in \([0,1]\) this is always satisfied except for the white image). In general, these functions form an open subset of the Hilbert spaces \(L^2(K)\) and \(L^2(\mathbb R^2)\).
  • It is not difficult to prove that two functions \(f,g\in L^2(\mathbb R^2)\) differ by a roto-traslation \((X,\theta)\) if and only if their cyclic lifts differ by the roto-translation \((0,\theta/2)\).

The Fourier transform simplifies as

\begin{equation} \begin{split} [\hat f_c(r).\varphi](u) & = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N} \int_{\mathbb R^2} f_c(X,\theta) e^{-i\langle r,R_{u-\theta}X\rangle}\,\varphi(u-\theta)\,dX \\ & = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N} \int_{\mathbb R^2} f\left(R_{\theta}X + \frac{centr(f)}{av(f)}\right)\,e^{-i\langle r,R_{u-\theta}X\rangle}\,dX \,\varphi(u-\theta)\\ & = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N} \int_{\mathbb R^2} f\left(Y\right)\,e^{-i\langle r,R_{u-\theta} R_{-\theta}(Y-centr(f)/av(f))\rangle}\,dY \,\varphi(u-\theta)\\ & = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N} e^{i\langle R_{2\theta-u}r,centr(f)/av(f)\rangle}\int_{\mathbb R^2} f\left(Y\right)\,e^{-i\langle R_{2\theta-u}r,Y\rangle}\,dY \,\varphi(u-\theta)\\ & = \sum_{\theta\in\breve N} e^{i\langle R_{2\theta-u}r,centr(f)/av(f)\rangle} \tilde f(R_{2\theta-u}r) \,\varphi(u-\theta)\\ & = \sum_{\alpha\in\breve N} e^{i\langle R_{2\alpha+u}r,centr(f)/av(f)\rangle} \tilde f(R_{2\alpha+u}r) \,\varphi(-\alpha) \end{split} \end{equation}

Some computations

Through the Fourier transform formula we can compute \[ [\hat f_c(r)^{*}.\varphi](u) = \sum_{\alpha\in\breve N} \overline{\tilde f} (R_{\alpha-2u} r) e^{-i\langle R_{\alpha-2u} r, centr(f)/av(f) \rangle} \varphi(\alpha). \] This immediately yields

\begin{multline} [\hat f_c(r_1)^{*}\hat\otimes \hat f_c(r_2)^{*}.\varphi](u_1,u_2) = \sum_{(\alpha_1,\alpha_2)\in\breve N\times\breve N} \overline{\tilde f}(R_{\alpha_2-2u_2} r_1) \overline{\tilde f}(R_{\alpha_1-2u_1} r_2) \\ \times\: e^{-i\langle R_{\alpha_2-2u_2} r_1 + R_{\alpha_1-2u_1} r_2 , centr(f)/av(f) \rangle}\,\varphi(\alpha_1,\alpha_2). \end{multline}

A simple computation shows that \[ [T_{r_1\hat\otimes r_2}(X,\theta).\varphi](u_1,u_2) = e^{i\langle R_{-u_2} r_1 + R_{-u_1} r_2,X \rangle} \varphi(u_1+\theta,u_{2}+\theta), \] from which we get \[ [T_{r_1\hat\otimes r_2}(X,\theta)^{*}.\varphi](u_1,u_2) = e^{-i\langle R_{\theta-u_2} r_1 + R_{\theta-u_1} r_2,X \rangle} \varphi(u_1-\theta,u_{2}-\theta). \]

This allows us to compute

\begin{multline} [\hat f_c(r_1\hat\otimes r_2).\varphi](u_1,u_2) = \sum_{\alpha\in \breve N} \tilde f(R_{2\alpha-u_2}r_1 + R_{2\alpha-u_1}r_2) \\ \times \: e^{-i\langle R_{2\alpha-u_2}r_1 + R_{2\alpha-u_1}r_2,centr(f)/av(f)\rangle}\,\varphi(u_1-\alpha,u_2-\alpha). \end{multline}

We have now all the ingredients necessary to compute the Fourier descriptors.
